The beginning

Age 4. The Reston Rattlers. Then the Whitecaps.

I was born in Washington DC on November 9, 1970, and raised in Reston, Virginia. I kicked my first ball at age 4, wearing the number 4 on the Reston Rattlers.

That number has followed me ever since. Sometimes deliberate, sometimes accidental, but it keeps showing up.

Reston, Virginia in the 1970s was one of the rare places where American kids grew up actually playing this game from the start. I didn't have to discover soccer as a college kid the way many of my generation did. It was in the air I was breathing in elementary school.

From the Rattlers, I went to the Reston Whitecaps, a legendary local team. The Whitecaps were my home club through the heart of my youth-soccer years.

The big brother chapter

Following Werten.

My brother Werten was literally awesome at soccer. While I was playing for the Reston Whitecaps, he was the Bellamy who starred at the travel-soccer level for Reston and McLean, then carried the game east to Phillips Exeter, and on for a bit at Princeton.

The way the Bellamy household worked: Werten set the bar. I tried to clear it. Watching him at the McLean, Reston, Exeter, and Princeton levels showed me what high-level American soccer actually looked like up close, by someone with my last name.

St. Paul's School

Illustrious JV life on the wing.

St. Paul's School, Concord NH, class of 1988. My illustrious JV life on the wing. Not varsity. Not anywhere close to a soccer scholarship. Just the kind of player who actually wanted to be out there, in the rain in October, doing the work.

New England soccer culture is its own thing. Fall sport. Played on the same fields as field hockey. Treated less as a national passion and more as a craft. I carried Reston into a New Hampshire chapter and the game changed shape with me.

The 1994 World Cup Draw

Las Vegas. The moment soccer in America actually changed.

I was at the 1994 World Cup Draw in Las Vegas with my dear friend Mike Morrow. Mike and I went to Harvard together. He now lives in Seattle, which means there's a particular cosmic joke that the second-to-last USA group game of 2026 is at Lumen Field, in his city, thirty-two years after we were in the same room in Vegas.

Twenty-three years old, both of us. The draw is where the matchups were announced for the first home World Cup since the United States had hosted a global soccer tournament. The room was full of Pelé and other ghosts of past Cups. The country had not made a World Cup since 1950 until Caligiuri's shot in 1989. Now we were hosting.

I have spent thirty-two years carrying that room in my head. The thing that happens when American soccer is given a real stage for the first time. This is the room I want 2026 to be the sequel to.

The EA Sports chapter

FIFA. The cameo. The friends who became Rockstar.

After Harvard, EA Sports. Intern in 1993, Rookie of the Year in 1994. The credit list includes FIFA International Soccer, 1993, the very first FIFA game.

And inside that game, there's a small detail that I will be honest about loving. I show up as a player named "Gordio Belamini" on Spain. An EA in-joke. My debut as a fictional Spanish midfielder on the original FIFA on Sega Genesis. The kind of easter egg you discover once and remember forever.

Three years later, Alexi Lalas International Soccer, originally for BMG Interactive. The upstarts from BMG Interactive who would later become Rockstar Games. Same soccer game, different masthead. Working with that team is part of the lineage.

Same era brought Three Lions as well, another BMG soccer title. Perfect Striker. And then countless hours playing Football Manager on my own time, because once a soccer person, always a soccer person.

I have a name on more soccer video games than most people realize, including this site. It is part of the journey.

Italy vs England, Rome, 1997

Stadio Olimpico. Working on Three Lions. In the stands.

While I was working on Three Lions for BMG Interactive (released February 1998 on PlayStation), I went to Italy vs England at the Stadio Olimpico in Rome on Saturday, October 11, 1997. A World Cup '98 qualifier. The match England needed not to lose to qualify directly for France.

The match ended 0-0. England got the point they needed. The image that lives in my head and in football history is Paul Ince finishing the match in a head bandage soaked through with blood, opened up by an accidental elbow in the 12th minute and reopened in the second half. Gascoigne later described his captain as "looking like a pint of Guinness." 81,200 in attendance.

To be in the room at the Stadio Olimpico during a qualifier of that stakes, while putting the final months into a game built around that exact team, is a particular kind of life-imitates-game-imitates-life moment. I have not had another night quite like it.

MLS, from the very beginning

Spartan Stadium. Man United tours. The 3252.

The first MLS match at Spartan Stadium, San Jose, April 6, 1996. San Jose Clash vs DC United. Eric Wynalda scored the first goal in MLS history. I was there.

The Manchester United tours through the United States in the years that followed. Pre-season summer friendlies. The first time a top European club came around to formally introduce themselves to American soccer fans on home grass.

Now in Los Angeles, the 3252 and my LAFC family. The 3252 is LAFC's supporters' section at BMO Stadium, named for its capacity. Drumlines. Tifo banners. The North End. The closest thing American soccer has built to a European supporters' culture and it grew in my city.

The supporter communities

Sam's Army. Wheeler's MLS Fantasy. PAX. AO weddings.

Wheeler's MLS Fantasy league. Mark Wheeler was part of the early Sam's Army era (the group itself was founded by Mark Spacone and John Wright in 1994). He ran a long-running MLS fantasy league. I played in it.

Sam's Army itself, the predecessor American supporters' group before the American Outlaws. I was around for that era. The fanzine, the singing sections at home qualifiers, the bus trips. That community taught me what fan culture meant at a national-team level.

The San Francisco Spikes, the gay soccer club in San Francisco. Pulling on a Spikes jersey is different than pulling on a college kit. The Spikes are one of the few American sporting institutions that already existed for me when I got there. I learned soccer-as-community on the Spikes.

PAX viewing parties. Game convention room, USMNT on whatever screen we could pull off a hotel TV. The crossover between gaming community and soccer fandom in my life is not coincidental. It is one community.

American Outlaws weddings. The supporter community grew up. The bus to the away game became the rehearsal dinner. The same chants in different rooms.

Why this World Cup, specifically, matters

A thread through every chapter.

This sport has been a thread through every chapter of my life. So many of you have been part of it. If you've been tagged on the Instagram post that goes with this page, you know which moment you were at, or which one we shared.

The last time the United States hosted a men's World Cup, I was 23 and at the beginning of my EA career. I did not have tickets to 1994. I was at the draw in Vegas, then watched as a fan with the rest of the country.

Thirty-two years later, the tournament is back on home soil. I will be at SoFi for the Türkiye match thanks to Pete Hines. I will be at Wiscago with the Francksens for the Round of 32. I will be in New York with Foster for the Final.

I got myself a proper USMNT kit for this one. Number 4. Because some things just stay with you.

See you at the matches. The beautiful game is about to have its biggest American moment yet.

Matches I've actually been at

The receipts.

Most of the matches I have actually seen in person have been club matches. MLS home games, Manchester United US tours, LAFC nights at BMO. National-team matches in person are the rarer chapter, but the ones I have been at are the ones I remember kick-for-kick.

The big national-team and event moments (in person)

  • The 1994 World Cup Draw in Las Vegas with Mike Morrow. Confirmed by my own words.
  • USA 2-2 Mexico, US Cup, Rose Bowl, June 16, 1996. 92,216 in attendance. USA in the light blue kit, the only time they wore it. Locked in by Gordon, 2026-05-27. Wynalda 34', Dooley 90'; Mexico through Rafael García 45' and Cuauhtémoc Blanco 89'.
  • Italy vs England, World Cup '98 qualifier, Stadio Olimpico, Rome, October 11, 1997. Working on Three Lions at BMG Interactive at the time. 0-0 draw. Paul Ince in the head-bandage. 81,200 in attendance. England qualifies for France '98 on the draw.
  • The first MLS match at Spartan Stadium, San Jose, April 6, 1996. San Jose Clash vs DC United. Eric Wynalda scored the first MLS goal in history (88th minute). I was there.
  • Freddy Adu's MLS debut for DC United, April 3, 2004, RFK Stadium, vs San Jose Earthquakes. DC United won 2-1. Adu came on in the 61st minute as a 14-year-old substitute, becoming the youngest player to appear in a major US professional sports league. An "uncharacteristically packed RFK Field." I was in the stands for the moment.
  • Manchester United US summer tour stops across various years. Confirmed by my own words.
  • USMNT matches in multiple cities with Quinn Hovey. Quinn and I have shared a number of US Soccer experiences across years. Specific city/match list still being pulled from email archives. He's at SoFi for the 2026 opener; I'm hosting from home. The next match in our shared US Soccer chapter is happening this June.

The Pete Hines Seattle USMNT watch (PAX timeframe, in a restaurant bar)

Pete Hines and I have watched a USMNT match together in Seattle, on TV, in a restaurant bar, during the PAX timeframe (Prime or West), in the past 15 years. Pete Hines is a proud retiree and a Wake Forest Soccer, Arsenal, and USMNT supporter. We have been watching together for longer than most people realize.

The PAX-Seattle calendar is locked: PAX Prime Aug/Sep weekends 2010-2015, PAX West Sep weekends 2016-onward. No USMNT match falls inside a PAX-Seattle weekend in that window, but several land within a few days of one and would have been on TV in Seattle while industry folks were still in town. The three most likely candidates for the bar watch:

  • Tue Sept 6, 2016 · USA 4-0 Trinidad & Tobago · WCQ at EverBank Field, Jacksonville · 5 PM PT on FS1 / UniMás. One day after PAX West 2016 (Sept 2-5). Best fit for a Seattle bar at end of con. Afternoon kickoff Pacific Time, USA winning a Hex match.
  • Tue Sept 5, 2017 · USA 0-2 Costa Rica · WCQ at Red Bull Arena, Harrison NJ · 4:30 PM PT on FS1. One day after PAX West 2017 (Sept 1-4). Marco Ureña scored twice. The Hex collapse becomes audible in this match.
  • Fri Sept 2, 2011 · USA 0-1 Costa Rica · friendly at Home Depot Center, Carson CA · 7 PM PT on ESPN2. Five days after PAX Prime 2011 (Aug 26-28). Stretchier on the calendar, but a Friday night bar slot.

Open question: which one of these was ours, Pete. Tell me and I'll lock it.

Email-archive evidence: Gordon and Pete were emailing about USMNT well before any of these candidates. June 29, 2011 thread: Gordon to Pete: "OH! and IDG approached us about supporting Quakecon, is that in your world now?" Earlier that same day: "My gosh on the flanks they were just plain better at soccer..." Pete back: "We're in bad shape when Freddy Adu is the best player on the field. I'm happy for him and how" (almost certainly about the June 25, 2011 Gold Cup Final, USA 2-4 Mexico at the Rose Bowl, the comeback-loss). So the soccer-talk friendship is at least 15 years deep. June 2018 thread: Gordon to Pete: "PS I am so excited for US v France this weekend.." (that was the USA 1-1 France friendly in Lyon, June 9, 2018, USA-pre-World-Cup-skip era). Pete and Gordon's email archive has a gap 2015-2018; the Seattle PAX bar watch likely lived in iMessage and in person, which is exactly the kind of thing it would.

The FIFA confession

I was a tester on the very first one.

One detail I have not put publicly until now. From an August 2025 email I sent to Christian Volk and Georgi Stoimenov at FIFA, after a kind introduction from James Baratta:

"My life with FIFA actually goes back to the original FIFA International Soccer. I was a tester on the [original]..."

So it's not just that I have a name on the credit list. I was one of the testers who put the very first FIFA video game through its paces. The cameo as "Gordio Belamini" on Spain was the joke that came out of being in that room when the game was being built.
